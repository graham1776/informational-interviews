The Interview Experience

Mastering the art of conversation that builds connections, creates value, and sets you apart.

The Three Phases of Every Interview

The interview itself is the best yet hardest part of the process. Anyone can get a person's contact info and send them an email. Much harder is actually meeting someone you have never met before, grabbing coffee with them and carrying on an hour-long conversation.

It will take practice, a bit of confidence and a good attitude. I promise it will be worth it. Remember, all you are trying to do is learn. That's it. You are not trying to impress them or sell anything to them. You are not necessarily trying to "get" anything from the other person. All you want is to learn, and anything else that comes your way is even better.

Every informational interview naturally divides into three distinct phases, each with its own purpose and approach:

Remember Your Purpose

Your goal during the informational interview is to learn from the other person. You're not there to impress them, sell to them, or pitch them your ideas. The primary purpose is gathering information and perspective that will help your career journey.

Beginning: Setting the Tone

You've arrived at the meeting location. If you've arrived early (you should have!), don't order food or buy coffee yet. Wait for the other person to arrive.

Once they arrive, exchange pleasantries. The initial small talk is key before you are sitting down across from the person. It helps break the ice and get a conversation flowing. Some good opening topics include:

  • Your common connection ("How do you know [mutual contact]?")
  • An article you saw about them or their industry
  • Simple questions like "How's your day going?" or "How was traffic?"

After these initial pleasantries, the conversation can follow two paths:

  1. They lead: They may start asking you questions right off the bat about your background and what you're doing now.
  2. You lead: You'll need to fill some "empty space" with your prepared 2-3 minute introduction about who you are and why you wanted to meet.

Your job during this beginning phase is to get the person comfortable enough with you that you can start asking questions and learning. This phase should last no longer than 5-10 minutes of your meeting.

Active Listening

Listen closely while they give you their background. Look for items to add to your mental list to ask them about later. They'll mention companies, discuss hobbies, talk about career choices, and drop many conversation threads you can pick up later.

Write these down subtly in your notebook to revisit during the middle phase.

Middle: The Learning Phase

Now that pleasantries and backstories are out of the way, the conversation should move towards you asking questions and them answering, teaching, and advising. This is the time to gather information unique to this person that is helpful to you.

This is a good time to let conversation tangents flow naturally. You may ask a question that gets the other person excited. Let that conversation continue before revisiting some of your other topics. Keep taking mental notes to continue the line of conversation if needed.

Finding What Makes Them Tick

If you don't find a topic that excites them immediately, cycle through your prepared questions until you find something that does. Some people like telling stories, while others like talking about the future. Some enjoy discussing industry trends, others prefer sharing personal career advice.

You can usually tell you've hit on a good topic by how quickly and enthusiastically they answer. If they immediately jump in with energy, you're onto something. A big pause followed by an "I don't know, I'll have to think about that one" is usually a sign to take a different direction.

Questions That Get People Talking

Here are some effective questions that encourage detailed, insightful responses:

  • What does a typical day look like in your role?
  • What's the most interesting project you've worked on recently?
  • How has the industry changed since you started?
  • What skills do you think are most valuable in this field right now?
  • What's one thing you wish you'd known when you were at my career stage?
  • What challenges is your company/industry facing right now?
  • How did you make the transition from [previous role] to [current role]?
  • What resources (books, courses, experiences) have been most valuable to your professional development?
The 75/25 Rule

Aim for them to be speaking about 75% of the time, with you speaking 25%. This ensures you're getting maximum value from their expertise while still engaging in a natural conversation.

End: Setting Up for Success

The End phase is the most important part of the informational interview, yet it's often overlooked. As your time wraps up (around 45 minutes into the conversation), you need to gracefully transition to the closing questions that will transform this one-time meeting into future opportunities.

There are several signals that indicate it's time to wrap up:

  • They check their watch or phone
  • The conversation has reached a natural conclusion
  • You're approaching the time limit they initially agreed to
  • The coffee cups are empty
  • They make statements about needing to get back to work

When you sense it's time to conclude, frame the conversation so they know it's wrapping up. Thank them sincerely for their time and insights, and mention 1-2 specific things you learned that were valuable to you.

Then, transition to what I call "The Two Magic Questions" (which we'll explore in depth in Module 7):

The Two Magic Questions

Before concluding any informational interview, always ask:

  1. "Is there anyone else you think I should talk to?"
  2. "Is there an opportunity for me right now at this company?"

These questions transform a one-time conversation into a potential network of opportunities.

If they say yes to either question, be ready to follow up with specifics:

  • For referrals: "That sounds perfect. Could I mention your name when I reach out to them?"
  • For opportunities: "That's exciting to hear. What would be the best next step to explore this further?"

No matter what, be sure to ask when you can follow up again. This opens the door to maintaining the relationship beyond this initial meeting.

Common Challenges and How to Handle Them

The Silent Type

Some people are naturally reserved or give very brief answers. If you encounter this:

  • Ask more specific, detailed questions instead of open-ended ones
  • Follow up their short answers with "Can you tell me more about that?"
  • Share a brief relevant experience of your own to prompt reciprocation
  • Try different topics until you find something they're passionate about

The Oversharer

Some interviewees will dominate the conversation, sometimes going off on tangents:

  • Use natural pauses to redirect the conversation: "That's fascinating. Speaking of X, I'd love to hear your thoughts on Y."
  • Keep an eye on the time and gently steer towards your key questions
  • Take advantage of their openness to ask deeper follow-up questions

The Busy Executive

If you're meeting with someone very senior who has limited time:

  • Come extremely prepared with focused, high-value questions
  • Start with your most important questions
  • Be concise in your own responses
  • Respect their time constraints explicitly: "I know you only have 20 minutes, so I'll be brief"

The Negative Person

Occasionally you'll meet someone who seems unhappy about their job or industry:

  • Listen with empathy but don't join in the negativity
  • Ask balanced questions: "What are some challenges in this field, and what aspects do you find most rewarding?"
  • Redirect to more constructive topics when possible
  • Take their perspective as one data point, not the definitive view
Interview Essentials

Always bring:

  • A professional notebook and pen (not your phone for note-taking)
  • A copy of your resume (just in case, but don't offer it unless asked)
  • Questions prepared in advance
  • A small exhibit of something you're working on (optional)
  • Business cards if you have them

Remember, this conversation is about building a relationship, not just extracting information. The more genuine your interest and engagement, the more likely the person will want to help you in your career journey.
